Reflection

In this project our group shifted away from looking for a client, and we settled on developing an open source tool for someone to use in the future, our open source tool being an upper limb prosthesis. This in a way made the task easier because it was less rigid, but also more complex because it was hard to think of where to start. Some of us at times even felt a bit frustrated with our lack of direction. But as the week went on we formed some good ideas, and developed some iterations which should be a good base for our future models. There are several occupational therapy tasks that we want to cover, namely: better weight distribution, individual finger movement, torque, and kitchen/general tasks. After doing our needs statement activity we came up with the following needs statement, “The child with an upper limb difference needs a way to make various finger motions so that they can hold and leverage objects.” To be successful we had to hit the sketchboard. We came up with many interesting design ideas, Gill discussed adding a torque factor with a ball and socket joint for individual finger movement, avery discussed using a gear, Johnny thought of water pouches for weight distribution (I thought a straw might be a cool plus, but it does not really make sense for the task we’re assigned), and I came up with a finger tool idea which would be a hole in the finger that got narrower as you got farther down the finger allowing for tools of various size, and allowing them to stay in place. If all of these sketches were successful our prosthesis would have individual finger movement, strong weight distribution, torque, and useful attachments. The tool add-on would harness the power of the individual finger movement from Avery’s gear, and the ball socket could be another way of getting individual finger movements, and quite possibly more unique ones. The pouches well not addressing the needs statement, do address an important issue, considering weight distribution head on (wrist on) will prevent the client from developing a righty reflex where they favor one side for leaning, and thereby make their spine crooked. Our first iteration was a semi-success. For our first iteration, playing off Gill’s idea of a ball and socket joint, we printed an arm model that we found online to see its pros and cons. Unfortunately many parts did not print well, and even the ones that did were too brittle for the screws that were required. To combat this we will try doing another iteration with PETG, if that doesn’t work we should probably avoid anything too reliant on screws. Another thing we realized is that the cuff was too rigid in the Phoenix hand, so we wanted to change material, and try TPU for more flexibility and comfort. It seemed like a great choice, and we will likely use it in our final project for the wrist, and various other parts. As for my finger parts, I finished the design in Fusion, and I’m waiting on my print. The water containers came out good for the most part, but we need to improve their tolerances, as the top does not fit well. The gear had a slicing issue, so Avery had to reprint them (no changes, just fixed the slicing error) then, she got the print for the gear finger, it was too small to figure out how they worked/didn’t work so she printed them again on 200%, then she saw that the hole was a little off, so another iteration would be useful, then possibly make the gear bit out of PETG so it doesn’t wear. We tried to print everything with 50% infill for consistency. We experimented a bit with PETG, but TPU was our favorite material that we experimented with. I think we learned the most from Johnny’s prototype because it gave us an idea of how things could slide in place, something we will consider moving forward in sleeves for tool add ons, more containers, and anything else that comes to mind.
